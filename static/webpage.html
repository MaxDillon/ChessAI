F. Scott Fitzgerald’s The Great Gatsby explores the implications of class and wealth, as well as their mesmerizing allure. One symbol that Fitzgerald uses throughout the story is Daisy’s voice. Fitzgerald’s many descriptions of her enchanting and musical voice demonstrate her social status, her money, and an inescapable attraction. Gatsby’s idealized image of Daisy is dominated by her voice. That same voice takes ahold of Gatsby, and draws him on an endless pursuit to his eventual death. Fitzgerald’s descriptions of Daisy’s singsong voice characterize it as that of a Siren: one that draws in men and then drowns them at sea. Thus, Daisy’s voice is a vessel for Fitzgerald’s theme that the promise of class and wealth, though alluring, is one of glamorous deceit.

Side

From the start, Fitzgerald establishes Daisy’s voice as one of high social status. This is apparent even in her first words: “‘I’m p-paralyzed with happiness.’ She laughed again, as if she said something very witty” (8). Despite her stumbling over her words, and her previous failed “attempt to rise” in order to greet Nick, she maintains her composure by laughing it off “as if” she had said something “witty” (8). This carefree recovery displays Daisy’s comfort with her class. The meaning of her first words further develop this air of class; she is so stiff from sitting on the couch, she claims to have paralysis. This reveals how little a woman of her social standing has to do, but to lounge on the couch.
